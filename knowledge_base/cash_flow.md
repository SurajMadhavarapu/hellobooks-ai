# Cash Flow Statement

## What is a Cash Flow Statement?
A Cash Flow Statement tracks the actual movement of cash in and out of a business over a specific period. Unlike the P&L (which uses accrual accounting), the cash flow statement shows real liquidity.

## Why Cash Flow Matters
"Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity, but cash is king."
A profitable business can still fail if it runs out of cash.

## Three Sections of Cash Flow

### 1. Operating Activities
Cash generated from core business operations.

**Inflows:**
- Cash received from customers
- Interest received

**Outflows:**
- Cash paid to suppliers
- Employee salaries
- Rent and utilities
- Tax payments

**Formula (Indirect Method):**
Net Income + Non-cash items (Depreciation) ± Changes in Working Capital

### 2. Investing Activities
Cash used for long-term investments.

**Inflows:**
- Sale of assets
- Sale of investments

**Outflows:**
- Purchase of equipment or property
- Purchase of investments
- Business acquisitions

### 3. Financing Activities
Cash from funding sources.

**Inflows:**
- Bank loans received
- Issuance of shares

**Outflows:**
- Loan repayments
- Dividend payments
- Share buybacks

## Key Cash Flow Metrics
- **Free Cash Flow (FCF)** = Operating Cash Flow – Capital Expenditures
- **Operating Cash Flow Ratio** = Operating Cash Flow / Current Liabilities
- **Cash Conversion Cycle** = Days Inventory + Days Receivable – Days Payable

## Direct vs Indirect Method
- **Direct Method** – Lists all cash receipts and payments directly
- **Indirect Method** – Starts from net income and adjusts for non-cash items (more common)

## Healthy Cash Flow Signs
- Consistently positive operating cash flow
- FCF growing over time
- Low reliance on financing to fund operations
